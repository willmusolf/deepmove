# DeepMove — Principle Taxonomy

Every coaching lesson maps to a principle from this taxonomy. Principles are categorized by game phase, tagged with Elo relevance, and include classifier trigger conditions.

## Opening Principles

### OPENING_01: Complete Development First
- **Elo range:** 0-1600
- **Description:** Develop all minor pieces (knights and bishops) before launching attacks or making pawn advances in the opening.
- **Triggers:** User made a non-developing move (pawn push, queen move, same piece moved twice) while having 2+ undeveloped minor pieces. Move is in opening phase.
- **Takeaway template:** "Before starting any attack, ask: are all my pieces in the game? Undeveloped pieces are pieces that can't help you."
- **Common at:** All levels below 1600, but especially 800-1300 where players start attacking with 2-3 pieces developed.

### OPENING_02: Castle Early for King Safety
- **Elo range:** 0-1400
- **Description:** Castle within the first 10-12 moves to get your king to safety and connect your rooks.
- **Triggers:** User hasn't castled by move 12 AND the center is open or semi-open AND there's no strategic reason to delay castling.
- **Takeaway template:** "Your king in the center is a target. Castling tucks it away safely and activates your rook. Make this a habit in every game."

### OPENING_03: Don't Bring the Queen Out Early
- **Elo range:** 0-1200
- **Description:** Moving the queen into enemy territory before developing other pieces usually wastes time because the opponent gains tempo by attacking it.
- **Triggers:** Queen moved to an advanced square (rank 4+ for white, rank 5- for black) before move 8 with 2+ undeveloped minor pieces.
- **Takeaway template:** "Your queen is powerful but vulnerable. Every time your opponent attacks it, you lose a move retreating while they develop for free."

### OPENING_04: Control the Center
- **Elo range:** 0-1400
- **Description:** Place pawns and pieces to control the central squares (d4, d5, e4, e5). Central control gives your pieces more mobility and flexibility.
- **Triggers:** User neglected central play — all pawn moves on the flanks, no pieces influencing central squares.
- **Takeaway template:** "The center is the highway of the chessboard. Control it and your pieces can reach both sides quickly."

### OPENING_05: Don't Move the Same Piece Twice
- **Elo range:** 0-1400
- **Description:** In the opening, moving the same piece twice (without good reason) loses development time.
- **Triggers:** Same piece moved twice in the first 10 moves while other pieces remain undeveloped. Excludes forced retreats due to opponent threats.
- **Takeaway template:** "Every move in the opening should bring a NEW piece into the game. Moving the same piece twice means one of your other pieces is sitting idle."

## Middlegame — Tactical Awareness

### TACTICAL_01: Blunder Check (The One-Second Habit)
- **Elo range:** 0-1400
- **Description:** Before every move, scan: is the piece I'm moving currently defending something? Can my opponent capture anything after I move?
- **Triggers:** User's move left a piece hanging (undefended and attackable) AND the opponent can win material. Eval swing > 200cp from a simple piece loss.
- **Takeaway template:** "Before EVERY move, ask two questions: (1) Is the piece I'm moving defending something? (2) After I move, can my opponent take anything? This one habit is worth 100+ rating points."
- **Note:** This is Studer's "leaky roof." If a player keeps triggering this, it's their #1 priority regardless of other issues.

### TACTICAL_02: Don't Ignore Opponent's Threats
- **Elo range:** 0-1600
- **Description:** Before executing your plan, check what your opponent's last move is threatening.
- **Triggers:** User played a move that ignores an active opponent threat (opponent's last move attacked something and user didn't address it).
- **Takeaway template:** "Your opponent's last move wasn't random. Before making YOUR move, always ask: what is my opponent threatening right now?"

### TACTICAL_03: Look for Forcing Moves First
- **Elo range:** 1000-1800
- **Description:** When looking for candidate moves, consider checks, captures, and threats first. These forcing moves limit your opponent's options.
- **Triggers:** User played a quiet move when a forcing move (check, winning capture, or strong threat) was available that maintained or increased advantage.
- **Takeaway template:** "Strong players always look at forcing moves first: checks, captures, and threats. These are the moves that put YOUR opponent on the defensive."

### TACTICAL_04: Back-Rank Awareness
- **Elo range:** 800-1600
- **Description:** When your king is on the back rank with no escape squares, be alert to back-rank mate threats.
- **Triggers:** User's king is on back rank with no escape square (blocked by own pieces/pawns) AND opponent has rook/queen that could reach the back rank.
- **Takeaway template:** "When your king has no escape square, a single rook check on the back rank is checkmate. Create a 'luft' (escape square) by pushing h3 or g3 when you have time."

## Middlegame — Strategic/Positional Thinking

### STRATEGIC_01: Improve Your Worst Piece
- **Elo range:** 1200-2000+
- **Description:** Find the piece that's doing the least and find a way to make it useful. Every piece should have a job.
- **Triggers:** User played a move with an already-active piece while having a clearly passive piece (low mobility, blocked, on back rank in middlegame).
- **Takeaway template:** "Look at your position and ask: which piece is doing the least? That's the one you should try to improve. A team is only as strong as its weakest member."

### STRATEGIC_02: Don't Trade When You Have a Space Advantage
- **Elo range:** 1200-2000+
- **Description:** When you have more space, your pieces have more room. Trading pieces relieves your opponent's cramped position.
- **Triggers:** User initiated a piece trade AND user had a space advantage (more pawns past the 4th rank, more controlled squares in opponent's half).
- **Takeaway template:** "You had more space — your pieces had room, your opponent's didn't. Trading pieces gave them breathing room they didn't deserve. When you have space, keep pieces on and let your opponent suffocate."

### STRATEGIC_03: Trade Pieces When You're Up Material
- **Elo range:** 1000-1800
- **Description:** When you have a material advantage, simplify by trading pieces. Fewer pieces means fewer chances for your opponent to create complications.
- **Triggers:** User had material advantage (1+ pawn equivalent) AND avoided an available piece trade that would have simplified the position.
- **Takeaway template:** "When you're up material, trade pieces. Every trade brings you closer to an endgame where your extra material is decisive. Your opponent NEEDS complications to come back — don't give them that."

### STRATEGIC_04: Piece Activity Over Material
- **Elo range:** 1400-2000+
- **Description:** Sometimes a very active piece is worth more than the material balance suggests. Don't grab material if it makes your pieces passive.
- **Triggers:** User captured material but the resulting position left their pieces passive or discoordinated while opponent gained activity.
- **Takeaway template:** "Material isn't everything. A bishop stuck behind its own pawns is worth less than a knight on a dominant central square. Before grabbing a pawn, ask: what does it cost me in piece activity?"

### STRATEGIC_05: Play With a Plan
- **Elo range:** 1000-1800
- **Description:** Every move should serve a purpose — developing, improving piece positions, creating threats, or addressing weaknesses. "Hope chess" (moving without a reason) leads to passive positions.
- **Triggers:** User played 2+ consecutive "nothing moves" (pawn advances with no clear purpose, shuffling pieces without improving them, prophylactic moves when no threat exists).
- **Takeaway template:** "Before every move, you should be able to answer: 'what am I trying to achieve?' If you can't answer that, step back and ask: what does this position need?"

### STRATEGIC_06: Don't Weaken Your King's Pawn Shield
- **Elo range:** 800-1800
- **Description:** Pushing the pawns in front of your castled king creates permanent weaknesses that your opponent can exploit.
- **Triggers:** User pushed a pawn in front of their castled king (h3/g4 for kingside, etc.) without a specific tactical or strategic reason.
- **Takeaway template:** "The pawns in front of your king are its bodyguards. Once you push them forward, they can never come back. Only weaken your king's shelter when there's a very specific reason."

### STRATEGIC_07: Control Open Files With Rooks
- **Elo range:** 1200-2000+
- **Description:** Open files (no pawns) are highways for rooks. The player who occupies the open file first usually gains a lasting advantage.
- **Triggers:** An open file exists AND user has a rook not on that file AND the move played didn't address the open file.
- **Takeaway template:** "Open files are prime real estate for rooks. Getting your rook on an open file first is often the difference between having an active position and a passive one."

### STRATEGIC_08: Prophylaxis — What Is My Opponent Trying To Do?
- **Elo range:** 1400-2000+
- **Description:** Before executing your own plan, consider what your opponent wants to do and whether you should prevent it first.
- **Triggers:** Opponent had a clear plan (supported by engine analysis showing opponent's strong continuations) that user ignored.
- **Takeaway template:** "Strong players think about BOTH sides of the board. Before making your move, ask: what is my opponent's ideal plan? Can I prevent it cheaply? Sometimes the best move is stopping your opponent's idea before pursuing your own."

## Endgame Principles

### ENDGAME_01: Activate Your King
- **Elo range:** 1000-2000+
- **Description:** In the endgame, the king becomes a powerful fighting piece. Bring it to the center and into the action.
- **Triggers:** Endgame position AND user's king is still on g1/g8 (or back rank) AND centralization was possible.
- **Takeaway template:** "In the endgame, your king is no longer a liability — it's a weapon. March it toward the center where it can attack pawns and support your own."

### ENDGAME_02: Rooks Belong Behind Passed Pawns
- **Elo range:** 1400-2000+
- **Description:** Place your rook behind a passed pawn (yours or your opponent's) — behind your own to support its advance, behind your opponent's to restrain it.
- **Triggers:** Passed pawn exists AND user's rook is not behind it (or worse, in front of it blocking it).
- **Takeaway template:** "Rooks behind passed pawns is one of the most important endgame rules. Behind YOUR passed pawn, the rook supports its advance. Behind THEIR passed pawn, the rook restrains it."

### ENDGAME_03: Don't Rush — Improve Before Breaking Through
- **Elo range:** 1200-2000+
- **Description:** In many endgames, the winning technique is to improve your position to the maximum before attempting to break through. Premature action leads to draws.
- **Triggers:** User had a winning endgame but made a premature pawn push or exchange that reduced the advantage.
- **Takeaway template:** "When you're winning an endgame, don't rush. Improve your king position, centralize your pieces, and only push pawns when everything is optimal. Patience wins endgames."

## Decision-Making / Meta Principles

### META_01: Time Management
- **Elo range:** 800-1600
- **Description:** Don't spend too long on forced or simple moves. Save your time for critical moments.
- **Triggers:** Time control data shows user spent very uneven time (e.g., 3 minutes on a forced recapture but 10 seconds on a critical decision).
- **Takeaway template:** "Spend your time where it matters. If a move is forced or obvious, play it quickly. Save your deep thinking for the moments where the game is decided."

### META_02: When Ahead, Simplify — When Behind, Complicate
- **Elo range:** 1200-2000+
- **Description:** When you're winning, trade pieces and head for a simple endgame. When you're losing, keep pieces on and create complications.
- **Triggers:** User was ahead but kept pieces on and allowed complications, OR user was behind but traded into a simple losing endgame.
- **Takeaway template:** "Winning? Trade pieces and simplify — your advantage is easier to convert with fewer pieces. Losing? Avoid trades, keep it messy, and give your opponent chances to go wrong."

|-----------|------------------------|
| Below 1000 | TACTICAL_01 (blunder check), OPENING_01 (develop), OPENING_02 (castle), OPENING_03 (queen early) |
| 1000-1200 | TACTICAL_01, TACTICAL_02 (opponent threats), OPENING_01, STRATEGIC_05 (play with plan) |
| 1200-1400 | TACTICAL_01, TACTICAL_02, STRATEGIC_05, STRATEGIC_01 (worst piece), STRATEGIC_03 (trade when ahead) |
| 1400-1600 | STRATEGIC_01, STRATEGIC_02 (space/trading), STRATEGIC_08 (prophylaxis), TACTICAL_03 (forcing moves), ENDGAME_01 |
| 1600-1800 | All strategic, positional emphasis, ENDGAME_02, ENDGAME_03, META_02 |
